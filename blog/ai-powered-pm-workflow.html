How I Ship 3x Faster as a Product Manager Using AI

Gonzalo Aguilar · 7 min read

I'm not going to tell you AI will replace product managers. It won't. What it will do — if you use it correctly — is eliminate the parts of your job that slow you down without adding judgment.

I've been integrating AI tools (primarily Claude, but also others) into my daily product management workflow for over a year. Not as an experiment. Not as a side project. As my actual operating system for shipping products in a high-stakes, regulated environment.

Here's what that looks like in practice.

The Problem With Most "AI for PMs" Advice

Most articles about AI and product management fall into two camps. The first is hype: "AI will transform everything, here are 50 use cases." The second is trivial: "Use ChatGPT to summarize meeting notes."

Neither is useful. The first overwhelms you with possibilities and no prioritization. The second solves a problem that barely existed.

The real opportunity is in the middle — using AI as a thinking partner that helps you move through the messy, ambiguous, judgment-heavy parts of product work faster. Not by replacing your thinking, but by compressing the iteration cycles around it.

Where AI Actually Helps (And Where It Doesn't)

After a year of daily use, here's my honest breakdown.

Where it's genuinely transformative:

AI excels at first-draft generation. Writing a product requirements document from scratch takes me hours. Feeding context into Claude and getting a structured first draft takes minutes. The draft isn't perfect — it never is — but it gives me something to react to instead of a blank page. Reacting is always faster than creating.

It's also excellent for pressure-testing copy. I'll feed it an error message, a notification, or a call-to-action and ask: "What would a confused user think when they read this?" The responses surface edge cases I'd miss on my own. Not because AI understands users better than I do, but because it generates variations faster than I can think through them.

Funnel analysis benefits enormously. When I have data about where users drop off, I can describe the patterns and ask for hypotheses. AI generates 15 possible explanations in seconds. Most are obvious. A few are genuinely novel and worth investigating.

Where it falls short:

AI cannot make product decisions. It can surface options, pressure-test assumptions, and generate frameworks — but it has no context about your users, your business constraints, your team's capacity, or the political dynamics of your organization. The moment you outsource judgment to AI, you've abdicated your core responsibility as a PM.

It also struggles with anything that requires institutional knowledge. "Why does this flow work this way?" often has an answer rooted in a compliance requirement from three years ago, a vendor limitation, or a decision made by someone who left. AI doesn't know any of that.

My Daily Workflow

Here's how AI is actually integrated into my work, broken down by activity.

PRD and Spec Writing

I never start a spec from scratch. I describe the problem, the user context, the constraints, and the desired outcome to Claude. It generates a structured draft with sections I'd use anyway — problem statement, user stories, acceptance criteria, edge cases.

Then I edit. I add the institutional context, the political nuance, the dependencies only I know about. The AI did the scaffolding. I did the architecture.

Time saved: A spec that used to take 4-6 hours of writing time now takes 1-2 hours of editing time.

UX Copy Iteration

This is where AI shines brightest for me. I'll have a registration error message like "Your information could not be verified." I'll ask Claude to generate 10 variations that are more specific, more confident, and less likely to cause user anxiety.

Then I evaluate them against my copy principles: Does it tell the user what happened? Does it tell them what to do next? Does it avoid hedging language? The best option usually combines elements from 2-3 of the generated variants.

Time saved: Copy iteration cycles that took a day of back-and-forth with UX writers now take 30 minutes.

Experiment Evaluation

After running an A/B test, I need to evaluate results. I describe the experiment setup, share the metrics, and ask Claude to help me think through: Is this statistically significant? What confounding factors should I consider? What's the business impact if we ship the winner?

This doesn't replace a data analyst. But it means I arrive at the data review meeting with sharper questions and a preliminary read on the results, which makes the conversation more productive.

Time saved: Experiment post-mortems are 50% faster because I've pre-processed the analysis.

Rapid Prototyping

This is the most underrated AI capability for PMs. I use Claude Code to build functional HTML prototypes — clickable registration flows, landing page variants, interactive demos. Not production code. Prototypes.

A prototype that would take a designer two days and a front-end developer another two days, I can generate in hours. It won't be pixel-perfect. It won't be production-ready. But it will be good enough to test with users, show to stakeholders, and validate or kill an idea before investing real engineering time.

Time saved: Concept-to-testable-prototype compressed from 1-2 weeks to 1-2 days.

The Framework: Augment, Don't Automate

The mental model I use is simple. For every task in my workflow, I ask: Is this a judgment task or a generation task?

Judgment tasks require context, experience, and accountability. Choosing what to build next. Deciding how to navigate a stakeholder disagreement. Evaluating whether a metric improvement is meaningful. These stay with me.

Generation tasks require producing volume — drafts, variations, analysis, scaffolding. These get routed through AI first, then refined with my judgment.

The mistake most people make is trying to automate judgment. They ask AI "Should we build this feature?" and expect a useful answer. That's not how it works. Instead, ask: "Here are three options we're considering. For each one, what are the strongest arguments for and against?" Now you're using AI to generate inputs for your judgment, not replace it.

What This Means for Your Career

If you're a PM not yet using AI in your daily workflow, you're at a disadvantage — but not for the reasons you think. It's not that AI will take your job. It's that PMs who use AI effectively ship faster, iterate more, and arrive at better decisions because they've explored more options in less time.

The compounding effect is real. If I can test 3x more hypotheses per quarter because I can prototype and evaluate faster, I'll find better opportunities and build a stronger track record over time.

Start small. Pick one task you do repeatedly — writing user stories, evaluating experiment results, drafting stakeholder updates — and try running it through an AI tool first. Don't expect perfection. Expect a useful starting point that you refine.

That's the entire secret. There is no magic prompt. There is no "AI product management framework" that automates the hard parts. There's just a faster way to do the work that surrounds your judgment — so you can spend more time on the judgment itself.
