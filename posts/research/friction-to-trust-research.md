### Stat 2 — Form field reduction and conversion uplift

**Data point:** The average checkout has 11.3 form fields, but most sites only need 8. Reducing fields and improving checkout design can increase conversion rates by up to 35.26%. Baymard estimates $260 billion in lost orders is recoverable in the US and EU through better checkout design alone.

**Source:** Baymard Institute, 2024 Checkout Research. https://baymard.com/blog/checkout-flow-average-form-fields and https://baymard.com/blog/checkout-2024-launch

**Where to insert:** Stage 3 "Design" section, under "Practical Trust-Building Swaps." Adds a concrete number to the benefit of simplifying forms.

**Exact sentence to add (after the "Replace: Generic contact form / With: Multiple contact options" swap):**
> The numbers back this up: Baymard Institute's 2024 research found that reducing checkout form fields from the average 11.3 down to the recommended 8 — and fixing other checkout friction — can increase conversion rates by up to 35.26%. That's not a marginal gain; it's a structural advantage hiding in plain sight.

### Stat 3 — First impression timing

**Data point:** Users form a visual impression of a website in as little as 50 milliseconds — and 94% of first-impression feedback is about design, not content. Stanford research found that 68% of users say a business's credibility is influenced by its website design.

**Source:** Lindgaard et al. (2006), "Attention web designers: You have 50 milliseconds to make a good first impression," Behaviour & Information Technology, 25(2). Confirmed by Google research on visual complexity. Stanford Web Credibility Research via Consumer WebWatch. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01449290500330448

**Where to insert:** "Trust Deficits" friction point under Stage 1 Detect, to quantify why first impressions matter so much.

**Exact sentence to add (after "New visitors don't know you from Adam."):**
> Research published in Behaviour & Information Technology found that users form a credibility judgment about a website in just 50 milliseconds — before they've read a single word. And Stanford's Web Credibility research found that 68% of users say a business's credibility is directly shaped by its website design. You're not just competing on product; you're competing on first glance.
