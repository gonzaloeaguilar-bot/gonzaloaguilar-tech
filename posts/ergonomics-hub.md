## The Pain Map: Where Developers Break Down First

If you code six or more hours a day, your body has a failure sequence. It's predictable, and it follows the same path for almost everyone. Think of it like a system with six failure points — ignore one, and it cascades.

**Wrists and hands.** This is where most developers feel it first. Your wrists sit in unnatural extension and ulnar deviation on a flat keyboard — bent up and angled outward — for hours. A CDC/NIOSH-funded study found conventional keyboards force 10 to 15 degrees of ulnar deviation and 20 degrees of wrist extension. That's the mechanical root of carpal tunnel pressure.

**Forearms and elbows.** Full forearm pronation (palms face down) compresses the tendons and nerves running through your forearm. Over time, this contributes to both carpal tunnel and tennis elbow. Most developers never connect elbow pain to their keyboard.

**Shoulders.** A full-size keyboard pushes your mouse too far to the right, which means your right shoulder is permanently abducted (reaching outward). That's a rotator cuff problem waiting to happen.

**Neck.** Monitor too low, too high, or off-center. Your neck compensates. The average human head weighs about 11 pounds, and every inch of forward lean adds roughly 10 pounds of effective weight on your cervical spine.

**Lower back.** Bad chair, no lumbar support, or just sitting too long without movement. The discs in your lower spine compress unevenly, and over months, you get that deep ache that never fully goes away.

**Eyes.** Blue light, screen glare, insufficient blinking. Dry eyes and tension headaches that compound every other problem on this list.
