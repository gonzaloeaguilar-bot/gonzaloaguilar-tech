Founder's PM Playbook

Product thinking for non-technical founders and small business owners. Map your customer's actual journey, find the friction eating your revenue, and build your first real experiment — no tech team required.

6 modules · ~4 hours of reading · Frameworks + templates · No sign-up required

Most business owners build what they think customers want. Product managers build what they know customers need — because they've done the work to find out. That difference in mindset explains a lot of why some businesses grow and others plateau.

This course teaches you to think like a PM without requiring you to become one. You don't need a development team, a data warehouse, or an MBA. You need a framework for finding out what's actually happening in your business, a way to test ideas before you bet money on them, and a method for measuring the things that actually predict growth.

Don't stress — I got you. Let's start with your customers.

Module 1

Your Customer's Journey — Mapped, Not Guessed

Concept

  • Understand what a customer journey map is and why it matters more than a website audit
  • Build a simple 5-stage journey map for your business
  • Identify the two stages where most businesses leak the most value

Why guessing doesn't work

Most business owners assume they know their customer's experience because they built it. This is the product creator's blind spot. You know how your product is supposed to work. Your customers know how it actually works — which is a different thing.

A customer journey map forces you to document what a real customer actually experiences from the first moment they hear about you to the moment they become loyal (or churn). It surfaces the gaps between your intent and their reality. And it almost always shows you something you didn't expect.

The five stages of every customer journey

Stage 1

Awareness

  • How do they find you?
  • What do they see first?
  • What do they believe at this stage?
Stage 2

Consideration

  • How do they evaluate you?
  • What questions do they have?
  • Who else are they comparing?
Stage 3

Decision

  • What triggers the purchase?
  • What causes them to leave?
  • What friction exists here?
Stage 4

Onboarding

  • What is their first experience?
  • Do they get value quickly?
  • Where do they get confused?
Stage 5

Loyalty

  • Why do they come back?
  • Why do they leave?
  • Who do they tell?

How to build your map

Step 1 — Pick one customer type. If you serve multiple distinct customer segments, pick the one that represents the most revenue or the one you most want to grow. Don't try to map all of them at once.

Step 2 — For each stage, write three things: What is the customer trying to do? What are they feeling? What could go wrong? Write from their perspective, not yours.

Step 3 — Validate it. This is the step most people skip. Talk to three real customers and ask them to describe their experience from the moment they heard of you. Their description will diverge from yours in at least two places. Those divergences are where your work is.

Where most businesses leak value

Based on McKinsey research on customer experience, the two highest-leverage stages for most small businesses are Decision and Onboarding.

At the Decision stage: most customers who leave do so because of something unresolved — a question that wasn't answered, a trust signal that wasn't present, a comparison that you lost on price because your value wasn't clear enough. These are fixable without spending money.

At the Onboarding stage: most churn begins in the first interaction, not months later. A customer who doesn't get value quickly doesn't give you a second chance to make a first impression. If your onboarding experience is a PDF, a generic welcome email, or a waiting period without communication — those are your highest-leverage friction points.

The Three-Layer Model

Simple version: Your customer's experience isn't what you designed. It's what they experienced.

Business consequence: The gap between those two things is where revenue disappears.

Real-world analogy: A restaurant owner who has never eaten at their own restaurant during a Friday dinner rush does not know what their customers know. Map the journey. Then walk it.

Key Takeaway — Module 1

A customer journey map built from customer interviews beats any amount of internal assumptions. The map isn't the territory — but it's the closest thing to a map of where your revenue is going.

Exercise 1

Build a one-page journey map for your business using the 5-stage framework above. For each stage, write: what is the customer trying to do, what are they feeling, and what could go wrong. Then call one customer you haven't spoken to in 30 days and ask them to describe how they became a customer. Compare their answer to your map. Note every divergence.

Module 2

Finding Friction Points in Your Business

Audit framework

  • Apply the Friction Audit to find the 3 highest-cost problems in your business
  • Distinguish between friction you own and friction caused by external factors
  • Prioritize friction points by business impact, not annoyance level

What friction actually costs

Friction is anything that adds effort, confusion, or hesitation between your customer and the outcome they came to you for. It doesn't have to be obvious. A checkout form with too many fields is friction. A pricing page that doesn't answer the three questions most people have is friction. An email confirmation that doesn't include the next step is friction.

Friction compounds. A customer who encounters 3 small friction points in your buying process may complete the purchase — but they're less likely to come back, more likely to complain to someone, and less likely to refer you. Friction doesn't just reduce conversion. It reduces lifetime value.

The Friction Audit framework

Go through every touchpoint in your customer journey (you built the map in Module 1) and ask these four questions at each step:

  1. Is this step necessary? If the customer could skip it and still get what they came for, it's a candidate for removal.
  2. Is the expected outcome of this step obvious? If a customer would reasonably ask "what do I do now?" — you have a clarity problem.
  3. Is there anything here that could cause hesitation or anxiety? Price reveals, data requests, form complexity, missing trust signals — all create hesitation.
  4. What does your support inbox or your reviews say about this step? Repeated questions about the same thing are a friction signal. Repeated complaints are a friction fire alarm.

How to categorize what you find

Not all friction is equally worth fixing. Prioritize by the product of two variables:

  • Frequency: How many customers experience this friction point? (High = lots of people)
  • Severity: What does it cost when this friction occurs? (High = abandoned purchase, churn, negative review)

High frequency + high severity = fix this week. High frequency + low severity = fix this quarter. Low frequency + high severity = monitor and have a plan. Low frequency + low severity = don't touch it yet.

The support ticket test

Pull the last 30 questions or complaints your business received — email, DMs, reviews, anything. Group them by topic. The topic with the most entries is almost certainly a high-frequency friction point. Fixing it reduces support volume AND increases conversion. That's a two-for-one return on the same effort.

Friction you own vs. friction you inherit

Some friction is in your control (your checkout flow, your onboarding email, your website copy). Some friction is outside your control (a payment processor that adds authentication steps, a shipping partner that causes delays, a marketplace platform with its own UX). Know the difference before you prioritize — you can only fix what you own.

That said: when you can't fix external friction, you can often mitigate it. Set expectations before the friction occurs ("your bank may send a verification code — here's what to do"). Offer alternatives when the friction is predictably high ("if you'd prefer to pay over the phone, here's our number"). You can't remove all friction, but you can reduce its cost.

Key Takeaway — Module 2

The friction audit is not a one-time exercise. Do it every quarter. Your business changes, your tools change, and new friction appears constantly. The businesses that grow fastest are the ones that remove friction faster than their competitors create it.

Exercise 2

Run a friction audit on your three most revenue-critical customer touchpoints. Apply the 4 questions to each one. Then score each friction point you find on a 1-3 scale for frequency and severity. Your highest-priority fix is the one with the highest combined score. Fix that one first — before anything else on your roadmap.

Module 3

Metrics That Matter vs. Vanity Metrics

Why it matters

  • Distinguish vanity metrics from actionable metrics for your specific business
  • Define the 3-5 metrics that actually predict whether your business is healthy
  • Set up a simple metrics tracker you'll actually use

What vanity metrics are (and why they feel so good)

A vanity metric is any number that goes up in ways that don't correlate with actual business health. Social media followers. Website pageviews. App downloads. Total registered users. Email subscribers (without engagement data).

These numbers feel good because they go up. They're easy to measure. They're shareable. And they're almost completely disconnected from whether your business is growing in a way that will still be true in 12 months.

"Vanity metrics make you feel good. Actionable metrics make you money." — Eric Ries, The Lean Startup

Vanity vs. real metrics — examples for small businesses

Vanity metrics

  • Instagram followers
  • Total website visits
  • Email list size
  • App downloads
  • Press mentions
  • Total registered users
  • Likes and shares

Metrics that matter

  • Revenue per customer (monthly)
  • Customer acquisition cost
  • Customer lifetime value
  • Conversion rate (visit to purchase)
  • Repeat purchase rate
  • Net Promoter Score
  • Time to first value

The three questions that identify your real metrics

For your specific business, your real metrics come from answering three questions:

1. What behavior most directly predicts revenue? For a service business, it might be consultation calls booked. For an e-commerce store, it's repeat purchase rate. For a SaaS product, it's weekly active users. Find the behavior that, when it goes up, revenue reliably follows. That's your leading indicator.

2. What is the single most important sign that a customer will stay? In product management, this is called the "activation event" — the moment a new customer does the thing that most strongly predicts they'll still be a customer in 90 days. For a gym, it might be attending in the first 7 days. For a software tool, it might be connecting a third-party integration. Find yours.

3. What does churn look like before it happens? Most customers don't churn suddenly. They go quiet. They stop doing the thing that engaged them before. Identify the behavior change that reliably precedes churn — and that becomes a metric worth watching.

Building a simple metrics dashboard

You don't need analytics software to start. A Google Sheet updated weekly with five numbers is infinitely better than a Mixpanel dashboard you never look at.

Weekly Metrics Tracker — Starting Point
WEEKLY BUSINESS METRICS — [Business Name] Week of: [Date] REVENUE Total revenue this week: $ vs. last week: + / - % vs. same week last year: + / - % CUSTOMERS New customers this week: # Repeat customers this week: # Repeat rate (repeat / total): % ACQUISITION How did new customers find you? (top 3 sources) Cost to acquire one new customer this week: $ RETENTION Customers who bought last month and bought again: # Customers who bought last month and did NOT return: # Churn rate: % TOP FRICTION THIS WEEK What complaints, questions, or drop-offs did you notice? [Free text] ONE THING TO FIX NEXT WEEK: [Single most important action based on these numbers]
Key Takeaway — Module 3

Metrics you look at weekly change behavior. Metrics you look at quarterly inform retrospectives. Pick 5 numbers that matter, look at them every week, and tie at least one decision per week to what they say. That discipline is worth more than any dashboard you could buy.

Exercise 3

Set up the weekly metrics tracker above for your business. Fill it in for the last 4 weeks using data you already have. Look at the trend lines. What's improving? What's declining? Now ask: if you'd been looking at this data weekly for the past year, what decision would you have made differently? That's your answer to whether this tracker is worth keeping.

Module 4

AI as Your Research Assistant

How to do it

  • Use AI to analyze customer feedback at scale — even without a data team
  • Build an AI-powered competitive research workflow
  • Generate actionable hypotheses from raw qualitative data

What AI can actually do for a small business owner

AI doesn't replace the judgment calls you make as a founder. It processes large volumes of text so you can make those calls faster and with more confidence. Specifically:

  • Pattern finding in customer feedback: Paste 50 reviews and ask AI to identify the top 5 complaint themes. It takes 2 minutes instead of 2 hours.
  • Competitive intelligence synthesis: Ask AI to summarize what customers say they love and hate about your competitors based on publicly available reviews. Gets you their friction map without the fieldwork.
  • Hypothesis generation: Describe a trend you're noticing and ask AI to generate 5 possible explanations and 5 ways to test them. You decide which explanation is most plausible — the AI just makes sure you've considered options you might have skipped.

The customer feedback analysis workflow

Customer Feedback Analysis Prompt
I run a [type of business]. Here is a collection of customer feedback — reviews, survey responses, and support messages. Read through all of it. [PASTE FEEDBACK HERE] Please: 1. Identify the top 5 themes in positive feedback — what are customers most consistently happy about? 2. Identify the top 5 themes in negative feedback or complaints — what are customers most consistently frustrated by? 3. Identify any specific language or phrases customers use repeatedly that I should be using in my own marketing 4. Identify any customer desire that currently goes unmet 5. Generate 3 hypotheses I should test to improve customer satisfaction based on this feedback Do not add generic business advice. Base your analysis only on the feedback I've provided.

The competitive intelligence workflow

Competitor Review Analysis Prompt
I'm analyzing reviews of [Competitor Name], a business in the same industry as mine ([describe your business]). Here are their recent reviews: [PASTE COMPETITOR REVIEWS FROM GOOGLE / YELP / APP STORE] Please: 1. What do customers love most about this competitor? 2. What frustrates customers most about this competitor? 3. What unmet needs are customers expressing that this competitor doesn't address? 4. What specific language do customers use to describe the problem this competitor solves? 5. Based on these reviews, what 3 things could I do to win customers who are dissatisfied with this competitor?

The hypothesis generation workflow

After you've analyzed feedback, you'll have observations. The next step is turning observations into testable hypotheses. Here's how to use AI to accelerate that step:

Hypothesis Generation Prompt
I've noticed the following pattern in my business: [Describe what you're seeing — a drop in something, an increase in something, a repeated complaint, an unexpected behavior] Possible explanations I've already considered: [List 1-2 things you've already thought of] Please generate: 1. 5 additional possible explanations for this pattern that I might not have considered 2. For each explanation, describe one low-cost way to test whether it's true 3. Which explanation would have the biggest business impact if true and if fixed? 4. What data would definitively prove or disprove each explanation?
The limitation you must know

AI analysis of customer feedback is only as good as the feedback you provide. It cannot tell you things your customers didn't say. It cannot verify whether customers' stated reasons match their actual behavior. And it will occasionally hallucinate patterns that aren't there. Always verify AI insights against your own direct customer observations before acting on them.

Key Takeaway — Module 4

AI is a research accelerator, not a research replacement. The founder who talks to customers AND uses AI to synthesize that feedback at scale is operating at a higher level than the founder who does either one alone.

Exercise 4

Collect at least 20 pieces of customer feedback — reviews, emails, survey responses, whatever you have. Run them through the Customer Feedback Analysis prompt above. Then take the top complaint theme and run the Hypothesis Generation prompt on it. You should end the exercise with at least one specific, testable hypothesis about what's causing that complaint and one low-cost way to test it.

Module 5

Building Your First Experiment

How to do it

  • Apply the Hypothesis → Test → Learn framework to any business decision
  • Design a two-week experiment that will give you a clear answer
  • Know when your result is meaningful and when it's noise

Why experiments beat opinions

Every business decision is a bet. The question is whether it's an informed bet or a blind one. An experiment is just a structured way to make a small, informed bet before you make the big, consequential one.

Most small business owners skip experiments not because they don't believe in them but because they seem complicated. They're not. An experiment has four parts: a hypothesis, a test, a measurement, and a decision. That's it.

The Experiment Canvas

Hypothesis
We believe that [changing X] will cause [Y to happen] for [this type of customer]. We believe this because [evidence or reasoning].
Test
We will test this by [specific action with a specific scope] for [time period]. We will only change [this one thing] — nothing else.
Measure
We will measure success by [specific metric]. We will call it a win if [metric] moves by [threshold] or more within [time period].
Learn
If it works: we will [next action]. If it doesn't work: we will [alternative hypothesis to test next].

A real example

A bakery owner noticed that their online pre-orders were low despite strong foot traffic. Instead of rebuilding the website, they ran an experiment:

Hypothesis
We believe that adding a "pre-order guarantee" message ("pre-order now and your items are guaranteed to be ready when you arrive") on our pre-order page will increase pre-order conversion. We believe this because customers have told us they worry about items selling out.
Test
We will add the guarantee message to the pre-order page for 2 weeks, starting Monday. We will not change anything else about the page or our marketing.
Measure
We will measure the number of pre-orders per week. We will call it a win if pre-orders increase by 20% or more compared to the prior 2-week average.
Learn
If it works: we'll make this message permanent and consider testing additional guarantee language. If it doesn't work: we'll interview 3 customers who visited the pre-order page but didn't convert.

The result: Pre-orders increased 34% in the first two weeks. The owner made the message permanent and now runs one experiment per month — each one building on the last.

How to know if your result is real

For small businesses, you often can't run formal A/B tests with statistical significance. That's okay. Instead, use these practical rules:

  • Run the test for at least 2 full weeks. Shorter than that and you're measuring noise, not signal.
  • Your threshold should be big enough to matter. A 3% improvement in conversion might be meaningful at massive scale. For most small businesses, look for improvements of 15-30% before acting with confidence.
  • Consider external factors. Did anything else change during your test? A holiday weekend, a local event, a mention in the press? If yes, your result is compromised. Run again under cleaner conditions.
  • If you're unsure, run a second round. Replicate the test. If you see a similar result, it's probably real. If you see a very different result, the first one was noise.
The failed experiment is not a failure

If you run an experiment and the metric doesn't move, you've just learned something valuable: that specific change didn't work for that specific reason. That's not failure — that's a cheaper version of learning the same lesson after three months of building the wrong thing. Celebrate the fast negative. It's more valuable than a slow positive.

Key Takeaway — Module 5

One experiment per month is the discipline that separates growing businesses from stagnating ones. You don't need statistical significance. You need a hypothesis, a clean test, a clear metric, and the discipline to learn from the result regardless of whether it went your way.

Exercise 5

Fill in the Experiment Canvas above for one change you've been thinking about making in your business. Be specific: what is the one thing you'll change, for how long, and what number will you measure? Write it down before you start. Then run it. Then come back in two weeks and fill in what you learned.

Module 6

Scaling What Works Without a Tech Team

Real example

  • Apply the Scale Without Scaling checklist before building anything new
  • Distinguish between operational leverage and product leverage
  • Build a 90-day experiment roadmap from your current findings

The most expensive mistake in growth

The most expensive mistake founders make is scaling something before they know it works. They find a channel that seems to be bringing customers, they invest heavily in it, and three months later they discover the customers from that channel have terrible retention. The metric they were watching (acquisition) was a vanity metric for their specific business.

Before you scale anything, ask: does this actually work, and do I have evidence of that? "Works" means: do the customers acquired through this method have good retention, good lifetime value, and good word-of-mouth behavior? If yes, scale. If not, don't — even if the top-line number looks good.

Scale without scaling

Before building anything new or hiring anyone, ask whether you can get more out of what's already working:

  • Can I reduce friction in the current process before I add a new one?
  • Can I serve the same customers better before I find new ones?
  • Can I document what's working so it runs without my daily involvement?
  • Can I automate the repetitive parts before I hire someone to do them?
  • Can I measure whether my best customers are having the best experience?
  • Can I identify what my top 20% of customers have in common and do more of that?
  • Have I fixed the highest-impact friction points from my Module 2 audit?

Operational leverage vs. product leverage

Operational leverage: Getting more output from the same input through process. Better systems, better documentation, better automation. This scales linearly — you're doing less per unit of output.

Product leverage: Building something that delivers value without your direct involvement. A guide that answers the 10 most common questions. An onboarding sequence that walks new customers through their first week. A referral mechanism that turns satisfied customers into salespeople.

Most small business owners focus almost entirely on operational leverage — working harder, working smarter, but still trading time for money. Product leverage is where businesses break out of that loop. And you don't need a developer to build it. You need the customer journey map from Module 1, the friction audit from Module 2, and the AI tools from Module 4 to produce assets that do work for you.

Building a 90-day experiment roadmap

Based on the work you've done in this course, you now have:

  • A customer journey map with identified friction points (Module 1)
  • A prioritized list of friction points by frequency and severity (Module 2)
  • A weekly metrics tracker showing your current baseline (Module 3)
  • AI-generated hypotheses based on real customer feedback (Module 4)
  • An experiment framework ready to use (Module 5)

Turn that into a 90-day plan:

90-Day Experiment Roadmap Template
90-DAY EXPERIMENT ROADMAP — [Business Name] MONTH 1: Friction reduction Experiment 1: [Highest-priority friction point from Module 2] Hypothesis: [from Module 5 canvas] Metric: [from Module 3 tracker] Duration: 2 weeks MONTH 2: Activation improvement Experiment 2: [How to get customers to value faster — Module 1 stage 4] Hypothesis: [from Module 5 canvas] Metric: [from Module 3 tracker] Duration: 2 weeks MONTH 3: Retention experiment Experiment 3: [What brings customers back — Module 1 stage 5] Hypothesis: [from Module 5 canvas] Metric: repeat purchase rate or equivalent] Duration: 2 weeks ONGOING: - Weekly metrics review (15 minutes, every Monday) - Monthly customer conversation (1 call or interview per month) - Quarterly journey map review (is the map still accurate?)
The compounding effect

One experiment per month that produces a 10% improvement in a key metric is compounding. After 12 months of that, you're not 120% better — you're 214% better. This is the math of systematic improvement vs. one-off changes. The businesses that build this rhythm early are the ones that look "overnight successful" to people watching from the outside.

Key Takeaway — Module 6

Scale what works. Experiment on everything else. The companies that grow fastest aren't the ones with the biggest budgets — they're the ones with the shortest feedback loops. You now have the framework for that loop.

Exercise 6 — Capstone

Fill in the 90-day experiment roadmap template above using everything you've built in this course. You should have enough inputs from Modules 1-5 to fill in all three experiments with real hypotheses based on real customer data. When you're done, you have a plan — not just a list of ideas. Execute it. One experiment at a time.

What's Next

You've built a customer journey map, run a friction audit, identified your real metrics, used AI to synthesize customer research, designed your first experiment, and planned 90 days of structured learning. That's more product thinking than most businesses apply in a year.

The next step is running the first experiment. Not planning it — running it. Pick the one friction point with the highest frequency and severity score. Build the simplest possible version of the fix. Run the experiment for two weeks. Measure. Learn. Repeat.
